In this case study I am designing the core elements of a modern concept news/media and 'endless scrolling feed' app, Readily. Much like outputs such as Twitter, Reddit, or Pinterest, I aim to find out what about an app makes people daily users.

Case introduction

In the saturated market of strong (social) media apps, trying to inject yet another app seems pointless. However, in designing such an app anyway, I hope to reveal many of the tricks and secrets of some of the best out there to see how they apply UX Design to keep their use traffic in the millions. Besides unraveling successful UX conventions, I also hope to design a few unique selling points to improve the experience further.

In short, Readily is visioned as a curated, user-content provided media app with an emphasis on written media, such as articles. An early goal, in contrary to the competition, was to avoid the sometimes overloading quantity of content on other sources.

Readily should be the place you would come to for an interesting, exciting, and relaxing read. It offering the user exactly what they want to see, on a platform that induces satisfaction, not addiction.

The problem

Many people use their smart devices as a daily source for news and other types of reading media. Access is not limited to physical location, allowing a user access wherever they go. Many people have their favourite app, which they use multiple times per day. However, there there is always the risk of poor heuristics and UX in media/reading apps, some of which some are hypothesized in the list below:

    Aiming to solve

  • Bad mobile integration of design can impede the user experience with distracting page elements, poor text to element size ratio, or sudden page loading shifts resulting in getting easily lost.
  • Many apps or sources can cause media fatigue, dulling the users' experience by the sheer quantity of content or the similar nature of many items.
  • Abundance of offer makes it harder to dedicate to and/or process a certain article or piece of information.
  • The reader feels as an afterthought, with monetization methods such as ads and sponsored content prioritized over a good user experience

Early goals

To figure out what makes a good reader UI/UX I will look at the competition on mobile. To get started, I've set my self a few hypothetic goals to chase:

  • Create an app that is engaging for the user
  • Create a pleasingly eying UI and a distinguishable style
  • Add unique features as an experience improver and a selling point
  • Be a platform for various media types

Research

Survey: What do users want?

Before going deeper into analyzing and looking at solutions, I asked a number of participants(n=25) to fill in a short survey about their preferred methods for receiving reading media. This way I could establish if there's a big enough potential audience for content in reading format.

    I asked the participant a few simple questions about their daily activities, to find out these things:

  • Does the participant access any media while away from home, and which method would they prefer for doing so.
  • Which out of various media type does the participant access most regularly in this situation.
  • Which app/source do they primarily use to access this type of media.

    Top 6 most commonly used apps as stated by participants(25):

  • Spotify (8)
  • Instagram (6)
  • Reddit (4)
  • Facebook (3)
  • Twitter (3)
  • Youtube (3)

    Analysis

  • Amongst participants, the most common device to leave the user's home is a smartphone.
  • The second most common is a laptop.
  • Many participants prefer to use their device to listen to music
  • However, social media is the most popular choice
  • Not many participants said to use more traditional sources such as news or e-books.
  • Although, it does seem many participants use apps that provide updated content on actualities or story- or media-based content in a similar way (Twitter, Reddit, Instagram, Facebook, etc)
  • This might have to do with users in general using their social media platforms as a primary method of staying in touch with the world nowadays.
  • While no participant said to prefer video content, Youtube appeared as an answer 3 times in the follow up question. Perhaps the definition was misunderstood.

    Insight

  • Most participants prefer short content in a continuous stream (infinite scrollers, auto-updated walls, etc).
  • They expect a social aspect to the apps they use
  • Actualities and social media are intertwined
  • Some participants still enjoy lengthier media such as a story, news or audiobooks

Based on some of these discoveries I have a better idea of how to approach building Readily, what to focus on, what the user would expect. I also know which apps or sources I could draw inspiration from.

Competitor analysis

To gain understanding of what it takes to design a socially enabled media and reading app I took a look at a handful of market competitors. What do they do to ensure the user comes back to their platform?

    Based on prior research findings and intuition I've selected various mobile platforms to test on these points:

  • General purpose and operation
  • Heuristic qualities
  • Sign up / Onboarding
  • Social features
  • Beneficial unique features

    Commonalities found in researched apps:

  • App navigation and primary functions is found on the bottom of the screen in a tab bar
  • The apps' core functions are commonly found as a button in the bottom right corner, within thumb's reach.
  • Secondary functions, such as account menu and secondary menu are found at the top of the screen and appear sliding in in an overlapping menu.
  • Tertiary functions are often displayed more prominently than secondary functions (for example, a 'Reading list' label v. 'Settings' icon), because of how well people recognize each as their function.
  • Most apps have a dark theme.
  • Signing up is convenient with Google- or Facebook accounts or an email address.
  • Apps have relatively little onboarding, letting the user learn by discovery.

With this information there are still a few things to keep in mind. The apps tested are all popular and well-established. They show a lot of examples of great design that Readily definitly should draw inspiration from, but there are elements of design that they get to have as big names. Such as the minimal function explanation: this because most people will already be aware how to use them or have learned over time.

Finding a balance between new and familiar is always a challenge in user design, as will it be now. Building timesaving features don't save anything if they are not clear to the user.

Design

Giving each item the card it deserves

Building a content focussed app, delivery is key to a good user experience. That's why I've decided to first focus on the looks and functions of a single item and design the way it will be presented to the user in a most intriguing way.

I've decided on a card like design to allow for flexibility in use and to add affordance in use. Also, testing to see the card work on various display sizes validates the layout of other parts of the app design.

Size of individual items plays an important part of the UI and UX experience. One of the goals being emphasis on quality over quantity, I've decided to design individual items larger than common; aiming to draw in the user's focus with larger screenspace property use, and allowing for more preview space.

The Card Design Disected

My goal in designing content items for Readily was modularity. I wanted to design a delivery device that could be used to display text, multimedia, visual elements, metainfo, etc. in a single container that would be functional on various screensizes.

Secondly, the card's design has to function as a preview of the full item. This helps to draw in users to an item they are interested in, or saves them time letting them decide to avoid an item.

App UI & Navigation

2 Core Interfaces

Readily offers two primary use experiences: Highlighted Content Viewer and Personal Content Viewer. While both presenting the same great content, each has a specialized focus to help the user find the content they'll enjoy the most.

Preview items before committing

To prevent fear of commitment, Readily allows the user to preview each item by allowing a peak into the content. It keeps operating the app fluid and allows users to easily decide on what content to dedicate their time on.

Putting functions and settings in the right place

The app settings, ranging from functionality settings, account settings, privacy settings, etc, should be in a convenient place, without cluttering the UI.

Categorizing each function between primary, secondary, and tertiary levels of need improves the layout design and helps the user with to find the right settings with little effort.

App Style & Prototype

Style system

A well-defined style system is important for creating a coherent and recognizable app. For Readily I designed a system of core rules, elements, pallettes, etc., for function and form.

Highlight viewer

The highlight viewer is a variation of the Readily default app state / homescreen. It shows a more general selection of content, opposed to the personal viewer, which presents content specifically fitted to the user's personal preferences.
