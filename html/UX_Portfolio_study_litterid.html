THE PROBLEM

In this UX case study I explore the potential of mobile devices and user interaction in the reducing and preventing of public littering. Data shows that public littering happens frequently, and close to 1 in 2 of people admit having littered at least once.

In a time during which the state of the environment is in peril, finding ways to help keep our world liveable are of upmost importance - LitterID is a concept that aims to contribute to that improvement.

The goal

At its core, litterID should be a benevolent data-collection service. It should rely on the public's contribution to collect data on outdoor littering and use that data to aid in cleaning our living spaces. 

It should do this by relying on a widely implemented solution of scannable QR-codes on most common packaging of consumer goods. Users can scan littered items, from which data can be derived, such as geographic occurrences, product type and brand, and even find common offenders.

Main Challenge

The primary consideration in designing the litterID app and system is its purpose of being a highly versatile and accessible app, being light-weight as well as minimally intrusive on the user, while enticing the user to engage with the app as well as its cause. Clear definition of the systems inner workings is important, as most of litterID's moving parts are invisible to the user.

Lockdown

Soon after starting this UX project, the outbreak of the Covid-19 virus occurred. The resulting lockdown placed many restrictions on the possibilities of conducting UX research and user testing, thus as a concept study I am working with the tools and options I am given. I will be designing and testing usability primarily based on existing similar apps and will test with more users during the validation process of the case.

Research

Research analysis

Littering is an everyday problem that we might have grown too accustomed to. However, it is still an issue that needs to be addressed and eliminated. 

    Key findings

  • Thirty percent of people in the UK considers littering an issue.
  • Littering is a huge cost on society.
  • Most fly tipping consists of household waste.
  • Most littering consists of wrappers or containers from food or consumer items.
  • Of people in the UK, 48% admits to having littered.
  • Smoking related products and food packaging make up most of littered items.

Insight

The facts don't lie: littering is still a reoccuring issue in practically all places. No one likes seeing their area littered, so the information here can be a motivator for people to want to do something about it.

Apps that could inspire the design

While there isn't a mainstream app in particular focussed on identifying and collecting crowd-sourced data on public littering, there are plenty of apps which have similar functionalities and purposes, such as generic QR-scanners, camera apps, community/social boards, and rewards apps. Another important aspect of the LitterID concept is using the app primarily outdoors, for which there are also many apps which could function as an example.

    Existing apps with potential to test

  • QR-scanner
  • Meetup
  • Pokemon Go

Analysis

Making sense out
of our research

With research gathered, putting it all in order is the next step. To be able to make decisions on what the finished app should be, I considered a few important factors in the early stages.

Setting goals for how the app will interact with the user, how it will treat their data, and deciding on the nature of data collection, preferably in a way that is centred on the cause LitterID is for, not the user's unrelated data. Also, who would generally have a part in the system and what is the everyday use case for it?

First introduction to the user

Generally, people can agree on one thing: no one likes litter. Although we should not expect that most people will want to start cleaning littered areas, no matter how bothersome the sight, are people willing to contribute a smaller effort to improve the environment if there is direct positive feedback for it?

A great way to get people to want to do things is to disguise it as play. Thus, implementing LitterID with a use system that adds challenge, rewards, and personal accomplishment would be essential to receive successful user engagement. As a result, this approach aims to spark hype, interuser competition, and sense of progression whilst using the app.

User Journey Map: The common use case

To hypothesize the average user's experience of LitterID, it is important to describe the way the app is most commonly used. By looking at every step of the useflow in detail, it becomes more apparent what parts of the experience to pay closer attention to.

It is important that the user is aware of the purpose of the app, what they can expect from using it, why they should use is, and what they can get for using it.

Establishing the players

As an app with a larger socio-environmental cause, it is not just its users that will interact with LitterID. To take on the issue of littering, all parties potentially involved with the problem should at the very least be identified.

In the diagram aside, I describe which parties might possibly be involved through LitterID, and how they would interact or benefit from eachother. There are also parties that would have no need to interact with eachother, or aren't legally able to.

At the core of navigation

As an app with quite the specific use case, people are very likely to spend a long time on the app everytime. Therefor, it is important that navigation is swift and to the point.

Introducing users with a brief onboarding process, after which the user has the choice to start scanning, engage further with the community, or further complete their personal details.

Sketching the mobile UI

Before really getting into the design process, rough sketches help to work out the functionality of the app's interface and use.

A center-focussed 'home' screen should display an overview of user status and social updates. From there the user can navigate into a more detailed account / profile section. Or they can go to the commmunity section, where they can find updates, local events, messages, special offers, etc.
