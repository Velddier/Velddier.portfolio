THE PROBLEM

In this UX case study I explore the potential of mobile devices and user interaction in the reducing and preventing of public littering. Data shows that public littering happens frequently, and close to 1 in 2Â of people admit having littered at least once.

In a time during which the state of the environment is in peril, finding ways to help keep our world liveable are of upmost importance - LitterID is a concept that aims to contribute to that improvement.

The goal

At its core, litterID should be a benevolentÂ data-collection service. It should rely on the public's contribution to collect data on outdoor littering and use that data to aid in cleaning our living spaces.Â 

The app as a way to pinpoint problem zones

It should doÂ this by relying on a widely implemented solution of scannable QR-codes on most common packaging of consumer goods. Users can scan littered items, from which data can be derived, such as geographic occurrences, product type and brand, and even find common offenders.

The app as a way for users to work together

There should also be exploration in possibilities of social interaction within the app, and whether it might have a possible effect on use and engagement. Consideration should be put on the extend of social interactive capabilities of the app, keeping in mind the feasability and actual need of such functions for the app to function.

Main Challenge

Important consideration in the design fase is its purpose of being a highly versatile and accessible app that is minimally intrusive on the user, while enticing the user to engage with the app as well as its cause. Clear definition of the systems intentions and functions are important, as most of litterID's moving parts are invisible to the user.

Lockdown

Soon after starting this UX project, the outbreak of the Covid-19 virus occurred. The resulting lockdown placed many restrictions on the possibilities of conducting UX research and user testing, thus as a concept study I am working with the tools and options I am given. I will be designing and testing usability primarily based on existing similar apps and will test with more users during the validation process of the case.

Research

Research insight

Littering is an everyday problem that we might have grown too accustomed to. However, it is still an issue that needs to be addressed and eliminated.Â 

    Key findings

  • Thirty percent of people in the UK considers littering an issue.
  • Littering is a huge cost on society.
  • Most fly tipping consists of household waste.
  • Most littering consists of wrappers or containers from food or consumer items.
  • Of people in the UK, 48% admits to having littered.
  • Smoking related products and food packaging make up most of littered items.

Insight

The facts don't lie: littering is still a reoccuring issue in practically all places. No one likes seeing their area littered, so the information here can be a motivator for people to want to do something about it.

Apps that could inspire the design

While there isn't a mainstream app in particular focussed on identifying and collecting crowd-sourced data on public littering, there are plenty of apps which have similar functionalities and purposes, such as generic QR-scanners, camera apps, community/social boards, and rewards apps. Another important aspect of the LitterID concept is using the app primarily outdoors, for which there are also many apps which could function as an example.

    Other apps tested

  • QR-scanner - Main focus as a utitily, simple to use, quick to start.
  • Meetup - Minimal social interaction, higher focus on event organizing.
  • Pokemon Go - Safety during use is emphasized, keeps track of location, gamifies real life actions.

Analysis

Making sense out
of the research

With research gathered, putting it all in order is the next step. To be able to make decisions on what the finished app should be, I considered a few important factors in the early stages.

Setting goals for how the app will interact with the user, how it will treat their data, and deciding on the nature of data collection, preferably in a way that is centred on the cause LitterID is for, not the user's unrelated data. Also, who would generally have a part in the system and what is the everyday use case for it?

Introducing to the user

Generally, people can agree on one thing: no one likes litter. Although we should not expect that most people will want to start cleaning littered areas, no matter how bothersome the sight, are people willing to contribute a smaller effort to improve the environment if there is direct positive feedback for it?

A great way to get people to want to do things is to disguise it as play. Thus, implementing LitterID with a use system that adds challenge, rewards, and personal accomplishment would be essential to receive successful user engagement. As a result, this approach aims to spark hype, interuser competition, and sense of progression whilst using the app.

User Journey Map: The common use case

To hypothesize the average user's experience of LitterID, it is important to describe the way the app is most commonly used. By looking at every step of the useflow in detail, it becomes more apparent what parts of the experience to pay closer attention to.

It is important that the user is aware of the purpose of the app, what they can expect from using it, why they should use is, and what they can get for using it.

Establishing the players

As an app with a larger socio-environmental intention, it is not just its users that will interact with LitterID. To take on the issue of littering, all parties potentially involved with the problem should at the very least be identified.

In the diagram aside, I describe which parties might possibly be involved through LitterID, and how they would interact or benefit from eachother. There are also parties that would have no need to interact with eachother, or aren't legally able to.

Making sure people want to use it

It's not about the scanning

Besides its main function being a utility app to collect outside data, LitterID should be more than that to the user. To get users onboard and engaged with the program, it is important that they have the right incentives and stimulations to do so.

One important factor of enticing users is by positive social encouragement. Including a platform of social interactions on the app takes obligation away from the data collection function, transforming it into a positive aspect of play and challenge. It turns the objective from an effortsome lonely experience, to a connection to community that the user can use to work together with others.

Show users how they are helping

Along with direct social interaction, the LitterID should also give users direct feedback, both personal and collective. Collective positive feedback would give the user a direct notice of how their efforts have improved the greater status of their area, for example by displaying statistics of litter decrease.

Personal feedback can come from a score reward system from which users can receive tangible rewards for using the app. It can be from interaction with other users, participating in community efforts, or even from the ability of personalizing the app.

the core the app

As an app with quite the specific use case and that is intended to use on the go, people are unlikely to spend a long time on the app everytime. Therefor, it is important that navigation is swift and to the point.

Introducing users with a brief onboarding process on first time use, after which the user has the choice to start scanning, engage further with the community, or further complete their personal details.

Color

For it's color pallette, LitterID uses an orange-yellow (#F7C86C) as it's primary color, and a dark cyan (#2C65A4) as its secondary color. In this case, orange-yellow functions well as a generally appealing tint, giving off credible and authorative, yet playful and energizing vibes. Paired with dark cyan it creates a great visual contrast for accessible app navigation. Together on a white (#ffffff) ground these tints create a coherent and harmonious appearance that is both recognizable and accessible.

Element Size

Element size is an important part of any interface design. LitterID uses 3 systems to create an accessible set of harmonious element sizes:

  • Following Google's guidelines for ideal sizes of interactive content on mobile interface.
  • Using the golden ratio as a scaling measurement, creating harmony in relations between elements of various sizes.
  • Using a pixel-grid divisable by 4 assures that all elements have minimally needed room to breath and are displayed perfectly.

Font- & Typeface

LitterID uses the "Sofia Pro" and the "Sofia Pro Soft" typefaces for it's interface. They are two modern typefaces with an accessible humanistic character and a widely applicable style for font in different sizes. "Sofia Pro" should be used for larger fonts, such as headers, graphical text, while the "soft" variant should be used for smaller fonts, such as paragraphs, buttons, labels and other interactable elements.

App UI Elements

Buttons and interactive elements in the app are signified by use of the primary color for primary tasks, or with the secondary color for secondary tasks. Preferably buttons have a colored surface with contrasting label and icon, unless the surrounding UI allows for simplification.

Scaleable UI

To deliver content and information, LitterID relies on customizable and scaleable panels in its UI. Each panel follows the element sizing guides and adapts to the width of the device.

The LitterID app explained

LitterID consists of three parts that together provide a coherent user-centric experience. From here the rest of the app can be easily accessed in a few clicks. The three main sections of the app are:

The overview section

This is the first screen a regular user sees when opening the app. Here they can get a low-density overview of their status, as well as notifications of important things that have been happening. If the user scrolls further down they can find more messages and information. From here the user can navigate to the two other main sections of the app or they can use the scanner.

The user profile section

Here the user can find anything related to their personal profile. They can find a more detailed description of their progress with the app, find messages received from other users, and find their use history of the app. In this section the user can also find the application settings.

The community section

This sections shows the user what is going on around them. With a location-accurate map they can see relevant information, like the locations of bins or littering heatmaps. They can get news about events, get collective feedback about the state of the LitterID effort, find other actively engaged users, etc. Most importantly, here the user can find groups of other LitterID users to join on events to help and/or socialize.

The LitterID scanner

As a primary function of the app, the scanner should be the easiest function to use and quickest to access. To ensure the scanner is always a tap away, there is always an overlaying thumb-button on the higher surface sections of the LitterID app.

In other sections, where the user might have different objectives, this button might not show or be replaced.

Scanning is effortless

The scanner is supposed to be used outside and to ensure the quickest and easiest user experience, scanning happens automatically whenever it can. It does so by visually recognizing objects and pinpointing QR-emblems, capturing them, and processing in the background. At that point, the user has already progressed to the next stage.

Avoiding system abuse

Users might want to try to cheat the system by using the app and scanner in ways it is not intended.

Ways they might do so are by scanning any scannable products, whether it has been littered or not. Another way might be by scanning products still in store shelves.

    Preventative measures

  • Location access is required to use the app.
  • To be able to use the scanner, the user should be outdoors and movement needs to have been detected within a certain period.
  • Reward should not exceed effort. Users feel the reward is enough to fairly use the app, but not enough to justify abuse.
  • The scanner is deactivated in supermarkets, fastfood restaurants, and other places that sell consumer products.
  • A learning algoritm learns what litter looks like, filtering out products that appear unlittered (like packaged products on shelves, in prestine condition, or indoors, etc)

Some of these functions are simple to enforce, while others would require significantly more consideration, both in cost and time. However, failure to implement such functions might result in the ultimate failure of the app, therefor significant attention should be spent on these aspects throughout later stages of design, development and testing.

Use flow → Interacting with other users

LitterID has a system that allows users to interact with eachother. These functions add an additional dimension of use to the app and strengthen the sense of a LitterID user community.

This community is something that would greatly benefit the level of engagement that the app (and therefor the greater purpose of litter prevention) receive from users.

Users can message eachother, creating an environment that keeps users focussed on a cause. This, rather than shipping off user interaction to general communication apps, which might just add additional steps for simple communication.

LitterID User accounts

If allowed, users can be found by other users in LitterID. Their identity is represented in a simple account page that displays their name, profile picture, app score, and allows users to message lightly customize their profile.

User achievements

Active users can receive achievements for their use of LitterID. These accolades will be presented on their profile for others to see, creating a additional layer of reward and competition.

LitterID Groups

With a goal of creating a community around the app, the function to form groups and create and manage events with emphasis on communal deeds is an important part.

However, there shouldn't be too much focus on the app as a social platform; the core purpose of the social interactive functions should be as a pathway to real-world interaction.

and joining groups

Groups (or crews) on LitterID can be joined by other users. The purpose of groups is for users to interact, engage, and help in real-life locations with others in their area. Groups are free to meet up for casual events, but environmental actions (such as cleaning and picking litter) are suggested as the main purpose.

Participation in groups can grant additional benefits to individual users, such as receiving extra rewards and gaining higher scores when attending group events. Group identity and membership should also add an additional flair to a user's experience with the app, adding custom elements unique to certain groups.

LitterID Preferences & Settings

LitterID's settings and user preferences can be found in the user profile, where it is still easily discoverable.

From within the settings, the user can edit their personal details with the app, change how the app treats their data, or delete their account.

Reflection

Why create an app to scan junk?

I designed LitterID because I wanted to create a mobile app that did something to make a difference. As a personal case study, I could just as well have designed a mobile app that was more common and certain in it's possibility of success. How can you even get people engaged in an app that scans litter? Finding out how to create an app that could get people to use it for that purpose was the main challenge of this case study.

Litter is one of those things that effects us all to an extend, but it is something we are always hessitant to deal with. No one wants to live in a mess and most of us don't want to clean up what isn't theirs. Despite this general consensus, there are still people who leave their houses to make a difference. They gather in their spare time with others to put on a safety vest and clean a place of litter that isn't theirs. I had an itching idea that there was a way to get more people active to this cause, without asking them to sacrifice their weekends.

LitterID intends to give everyone the ability to help. Coming up with a way to get people engaged was a challenge for which I was able to find a solution in the form of gamification, user community, and reward systems. Finding a balance between these functions and aspects was important, as the app had to focus on each function evenly, not to overshadow the others.

In the end I am happy with the results: LitterID is an app that aims to connect people in fighting a common annoyance, together. It gives people who did not think they could make a difference a way to contribute what they can.
