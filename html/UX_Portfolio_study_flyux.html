Introduction

As part of my UX Design certification, I designed a web app called FlyUX: a fictitious new airline start-up with user experience highly prioritized.

In this case study I present my research, explain my choices in design and highlighting my methods. I applied an agile UX process to find and execute improvements to the current air travel booking experience. 

The primary goal of this study is to see if there is still space for improvements in a well-saturated market, while focussing on the user's experiences and necessities, keeping in mind the identity as a commerce/service company.

Setup

To establish a case, I had to first figure out what problem I could solve or what experience I could improve on. I started by looking at the competition: EasyJet, Norwegian, AsiaAir, and SkyScanner.

I studied the flow of these sites and I noted some of the conventions. This gave me an idea of what to focus on during research and when producing a solution.

Furthermore, I planned on doing a survey to get the general user consensus, as well as focussing on specific needs and demands with one on one usability testing.

Research

User Survey - 42 participants

To start gathering user data, I put together a 9-question survey about people's online booking needs, preferences, and behaviours.

Open all survey data in new tab...
    Discoveries
     
  • The survey showed results from forty-two participants experienced with air travel. They shared insight on their behaviours, context, and needs.
  • Price and comfort are balanced: some prefer a low price with less comfort, others prefer a little more comfort for a little extra in cost.
  • People most often fly with others, even in bigger groups.
  • Airline websites are primarily used for the purpose of booking a flight.
  • Most extras are overlooked and unneeded.

Insight

Air travel is increasingly playing a smaller part of the customer's holiday experience. It is a way to get from A to B.

Usability Testing

For my qualitative research I focussed on the experience of 3 participant: Janet, David, and Ben (persona names). These users provided a good representation of the various levels of experience, unique needs, (financial) consideration, and different co-passenger arrangements. 

See all collected usability testing data
    Discoveries
     
  • Janet is a middle-aged woman with a large family, who takes the responsibility to book many tickets at once.
  • Ben is a professional with a young family, whose main concerns are a good deal and efficient travel.
  • David is married with no children and is willing to spend for an improved experience on his flights to fulfil his and his wife's needs.
    Insight:
     
  • The website's and booking process' visual clarity and direction is important to many users.
  • Users are aware of company tricks to charge more money.
  • Since most users come to the website with a determined goal, too many process obstructions are distracting and annoying.
  • Users like flexibility in selecting and changing their flights.
  • If the price is fair, users will want to pay a small fee for improved experiences.

Analysis

Affinity Diagrams

With data gathered from researching the competition, interviewing users, conducting surveys and doing/analysing usability tests, I put together an affinity diagram, placing all pieces of insight and information on individual post-its. 

I then arranged them into sensible groups, focusing on user needs/goals/behaviours, site heuristics, conventions in style and function, and more, which you can observe in the complete diagram linked below.

User Flows & Personas

At this point I had a good understanding of the types of users I wanted to focus on, and I documented this understanding in 3 user flows and personas. Each describe a different user during the same flow of operation. 

I took the opportunity to address users' goals, context, pain points and likes. I also added a few notes of potential implementations and solutions for the final design.

Design

Workflow drafts

The first step I took in designing a solution was with pen and paper. I decided to focus on some more detailed app functions and a closer level of operation, to then have a seed to build the rest of the website around.

I sketched out a rough but descriptive flow map for the booking form operation and the flight listings / search results operation, addressing all routes of the progress.

Once all important section flows were sketched out, I transferred the most important ones to diagrams with Draw.io.

Site Structure & IA

Once having an idea of the low-level flow, I was able to reflect the high-level flow of the site's structure and information architecture in three ways:

1. Tree diagram - A standard tree diagram showing the top levels of site navigation.

2. Venn diagram - The Venn diagram shows the same information as the tree but aims to visualize the relationships between sections.

3. The sales and goodwill diagram - In this timeline I determined the best opportunities of sales and building goodwill during the booking process flow. It helped to get an idea when to present certain elements during the booking process.

Reflection

FlyUX was a corner stone project for me, the biggest project I've done thus far, entwined in many new UX methods. In the process, I learned new research methods that I can apply to future UX projects with ease. Most importantly, I got the hang of working on a project closely following a set process, which helped tremendously with the project organization and timeline. 

What I would improve on in future projects is flexible design, such as modular design or reusable motifs. Besides that, during this project I added many tools to my UX belt which I will carry on using in my future projects.
